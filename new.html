Hadley Leggett
Writer, reader, mother of three.

I should have realized I wasn’t cut out to be a doctor on my first day in anatomy lab, when I sat down on a wooden crate and discovered my seat was filled with hemisected human heads. I nearly passed out—on that day, and also about 37 more times throughout my illustrious medical career. But I persevered through four miserable years, only to graduate still dreaming of becoming a writer.

I'd fallen in love with storytelling as a child, but as a people-pleaser born into a family of scientists, it never occured to me I could become a writer. That would be like aspiring to be a Hollywood actress, or a mermaid-unicorn-fairy princess. So I dutifully earned my bachelor’s degree in biochemistry and went to medical school, like any good literature-lover would do.

After leaving medicine, it took me ten more years—and another graduate program, this time in science journalism—to learn to trust myself and my writing dreams. In May 2018, I enrolled in my first fiction class at Hugo House. I’d finally found my home.

Writer.

My recent work has been published in Literary Mama, 50-Word Stories, and Halfway Down the Stairs, and you can find my science wr I was selected for the AWP Writer-to-Writer mentorship program in February 2020, and my first novel manuscript was selected for Round 7 of Author Mentor Match.

A Better Mother tells the story of a struggling widow who loses her daughters to foster care and will do anything to get them back. More than a custody battle, it’s a story of loss and redemption, and finding help in unexpected places.

Reader.

I firmly believe you can’t be a writer without being a reader first. I try to read at least one novel per week, and I’m a subscriber of way too many lit mags. I’m also a fiction reader for Longleaf Review, and a beta reader for Spun Yarn.

Mother of three.

Mothering breaks me down and builds me up in ways I never expected, and my role as a mom is integral to my life as a writer. I wrote the rough draft of my novel in three-hour chunks, while my youngest daughter was in preschool, and these days I squeeze in revisions between packing lunches and folding laundry. But my kids keep me sane through the inevitable stream of rejections and disappointments in my writing life. And whenever I start to take myself too seriously, I go build a Lego castle.
